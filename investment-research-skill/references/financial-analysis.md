# Financial Analysis

## Balance Sheet First

Core question: can the company survive and are the assets real?

Check:

- Cash availability and restricted cash.
- Interest-bearing debt and maturity structure.
- Receivables aging, concentration, and allowance.
- Inventory composition, turnover, and write-down risk.
- Fixed assets and construction in progress versus revenue growth.
- Goodwill and impairment risk.
- Contract liabilities or deferred revenue as future revenue support.

Useful metrics:

- Debt-to-assets.
- Net interest-bearing debt ratio.
- Cash ratio.
- Receivable days.
- Inventory days.
- Cash conversion cycle.
- Fixed assets plus construction in progress as percentage of total assets.

## Cash Flow Statement Second

Core question: does profit become cash?

Check:

- CFO, CFI, CFF pattern.
- CFO versus net income.
- Cash received from customers versus revenue.
- Free cash flow after capital expenditure.
- Maintenance capex versus growth capex.
- Dividends and buybacks versus FCF.
- Whether financing cash flow covers operating or investment gaps.

Useful metrics:

- Cash conversion = CFO / net income.
- Sales cash collection = cash received from customers / revenue.
- FCF = CFO - capital expenditure.
- CFO / short-term interest-bearing debt.
- CFO / total interest-bearing debt.

## Income Statement Last

Core question: are profits from core operations and are they durable?

Check:

- Revenue growth source: volume, price, acquisition, accounting change.
- Gross margin trend and cause.
- Sales, admin, R&D, and finance expense ratios.
- Non-recurring gains as percentage of net income.
- Core operating profit versus CFO.
- ROE driver: margin, turnover, or leverage.

## Cross-Statement Red Flags

- Revenue and profit grow while CFO deteriorates.
- Receivables or inventory grow faster than revenue.
- High reported cash and high borrowing coexist without clear explanation.
- Contract liabilities fall while revenue grows aggressively.
- Capex rises without future revenue or margin improvement.
- Profit depends materially on fair-value changes, subsidies, or asset disposal.
